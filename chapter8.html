Chapter 8 of The Great Gatsby is an extremely significant chapter. It starts off with Nick meeting Gatsby who waited all night for Daisy. She however, never came out of her house. Gatsby is utterly distraught at this, because everything he has worked for has been for her, and in turn, in vain. Nick leaves him to collect his thoughts. After contemplating the events combined with a revelation from the billboard, he is determined to find Myrtle's killer, eventually ending up at Gatsby's mansion. However, both Gatsby and Wilson are dead due to a murder-suicide caused by Wilson's rage. Nick takes a moment to grieve, pondering over Gatsby's final thoughts.

Conflicts

Jay Gatsby & His Identity

Throughout the novel, although mysterious, Gatsby has always retained a reputable identity whether false or true. In chapter 8, Gatsby himself reveals when he first met Daisy, he had to equate himself to her to make himself seem worthy. After lying about his background, he continues to strive to retain his image all to keep Daisy. Five years later, he is only in love with the idea of Daisy and refuses to let go of her.

Themes

The American Dream is hollow

This chapter primarily confirms the consequences of chasing the American Dream, which Nick gets to see for himself when Gatsby further elaborates on his life. Nick witnesses a man who has built himself up from nothing, and although he has achieved his goals he is still empty without the one thing he wanted the most: Daisy. Wealth and Daisy are both equal goals in Gatsby's mind, and now he is torn because he cannot win them both. Gatsby's American Dream has dissolved into an unreachable goal.
